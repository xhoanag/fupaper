\section{Conclusion}

This study demonstrates that the evaluation of RAG-based chatbots in an educational context can only be adequately assessed through a multidimensional approach. The results illustrate that individual evaluation methods systematically capture different dimensions of quality and, when used in isolation, lead to distorted conclusions. Automated methods such as RAGAS enable a scalable and differentiated analysis of retrieval and generation quality, but underestimate didactic aspects and complex forms of interaction. In contrast, expert evaluations provide nuanced insights into pedagogical quality and learning effectiveness, but are resource-intensive and potentially subjective. User-based preference measurements capture perceived quality but do not necessarily reflect interactions that promote learning. The cost analysis complements these perspectives with a business management dimension and makes it clear that gains in quality are always accompanied by increased resource consumption and latency. It is therefore essential to recognize that only a consistently hybrid evaluation—one that integrates technical, pedagogical, user-centered, and economic dimensions—can yield a robust overall assessment. At the same time, it becomes apparent that the choice of evaluation methodology itself has a significant influence on the results and is thus an integral part of the system evaluation. Furthermore, realistic, usage-based datasets prove to be decisive, while benchmark-based approaches inadequately represent key application scenarios.

From an architectural perspective, the results confirm the transition from static to adaptive RAG systems, with routing mechanisms in particular enabling context-dependent gains in efficiency and quality. At the same time, clear trade-offs emerge between increased system complexity, costs, and perceived quality, necessitating the selective and application-specific integration of advanced components.

Several avenues for future research emerge. First, the further development of dialogic, learning-oriented forms of interaction—particularly Socratic approaches—appears promising, as these have so far only been evaluable through limited automation. Second, there is a need for domain-specific evaluation frameworks that systematically assess pedagogical quality and go beyond existing metrics. Third, future research should examine whether RAG is truly the gold standard for educational chatbots, or whether for general knowledge questions LLM-only approaches can already provide sufficient quality despite persistent hallucination risk.

The limitations of this study lie, in particular, in its limited generalizability due to the specific application context (AI Campus), the limited number of system variants examined, and methodological constraints inherent in individual evaluation approaches. The evaluation is based predominantly on single-turn interactions, which means that complex dialogic learning processes are only partially captured. Despite these limitations, the study provides well-founded empirical and methodological contributions to the development and evaluation of RAG-based educational systems.
