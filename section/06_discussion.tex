\subsection{Reflection of the Evaluation Approach}

This study combines four evaluation approaches: automated evaluation (RAGAS), expert and user evaluation as well as cost analysis to assess the quality of an RAG-based chatbot in an educational context as comprehensively as possible. This multidimensional approach aligns with recent research emphasizing that RAG evaluation must move beyond isolated component metrics toward system-level and application-oriented assessment \autocite{Yu2024RAGEvalSurvey,bdcc9120320}. This is particularly necessary in the field of education, as didactic and user-centered quality dimensions play a central role here alongside purely technical metrics. The results show that each method has specific, partially distinct strengths and limitations. However, using them in combination, as proposed in this paper, is an effective way to mitigate the shortcomings of each individual method, as their specific weaknesses become less significant when considered collectively.

Automated evaluation using RAGAS offers advantages primarily in terms of scalability and comparability. Through the standardized calculation of metrics such as Answer Relevancy, Context Relevance and Faithfulness, large volumes of interactions can be efficiently evaluated which reflects current trends toward LLM-based evaluation approaches \autocite{es2025ragas,saadfalcon2024ares,CRUDRAG}. The evaluation along the pipeline dimensions of RAG systems allows for a differentiated judgement concerning performance which does not only consider the final answer. At the same time, a central problem of the RAGAS evaluation lies in the fact that it is primarily based on semantic similarities and thus primarily evaluates classic question-answer scenarios \autocite{Lewis2020_RAG}. Educationally relevant aspects such as didactic structure, comprehensibility, activation of prior knowledge, or the promotion of reflection, are not adequately captured using this method. For example, intentionally simplified or abstracted responses can be pedagogically sound, even though they may appear less precise when evaluated purely in semantic terms. Similarly, RAGAS's automated metrics may incorrectly rate negatively additional contextual information or explanatory elements that promote understanding. This shows that robust automated evaluation in an educational context requires either finely tuned, domain-specific models or alternative approaches, such as ground-truth-based evaluations, even if these are more labour-intensive.
Furthermore, complex forms of interaction, particularly the Socratic learning assistant developed in this work, cannot be adequately evaluated using the automated RAGAS approach. However, it is precisely this form of dialogic, step-by-step knowledge transfer that represents a key added value of AI systems in an educational context. The sole use of automated metrics would therefore lead to a systematic underestimation of such functions.

Expert evaluation addresses precisely this forementioned gap and serves as an essential complement for a hybrid evaluation \autocite{bdcc9120320, 202504.0369}. By involving experts with backgrounds in both pedagogy and the subject matter, even complex interactions and qualitative aspects of system use can be assessed. In particular, the combination of standardized evaluations and think-aloud protocols enables a deeper understanding of perceived system quality, aligning with research emphasizing the importance of user-centered and context-aware evaluation in educational AI systems \autocite{ZawackiRichter2019}. The experts are able to evaluate answers not only in terms of their factual accuracy but also with regard to their didactic suitability and their contribution to the learning process. This is of central importance in an educational context, as the focus here is not only on the transmission of information but also on learning support. At the same time, it must be noted that this method involves a significant amount of effort and is subject to potential biases, for example, expert bias.

The user blind assessment in the form of a Chatbot Arena effectively supplements the evaluation approach, as it captures user preference signals under controlled conditions with relatively little effort compared to expert evaluation.
While automated metrics like RAGAS struggle with qualitative dimensions, human evaluators naturally assess aspects such as tone, structural clarity, and overall comprehensibility.
However, interpreting these preferences in an educational context requires caution.
The arena measures perceived response quality rather than actual learning outcomes.
Evaluators may favor responses that are confidently formulated or that are confidently formulated or structurally verbose, a cognitive bias in human preference testing of large language models documented e.g. by \autocite{Zheng2023LMSYSArena}.
Furthermore, users often prefer direct solutions, even though a more indirect, Socratic approach, in which the system acts as a scaffold rather than providing answers, has been shown to offer significantly greater pedagogical value \autocite{KASNECI2023102274}.
This value is better captured using the expert evaluation approach.

As with the other evaluation methods, the Arena's methodological design presents specific limitations.
Relying on a predetermined set of single-turn questions ensures internal validity and allows for standardized A/B comparisons, but it limits external validity.
Restricting users from freely prompting the system prevents the evaluation of the model under fully realistic, dynamic conditions.
Most importantly, the single-turn, context-free design precludes the assessment of continuous dialogue and conversational memory.
As \autocite{Deriu2021} note, static single-turn evaluations are often inadequate for capturing the performance of multi-turn conversational systems.
While the structured Arena provides a necessary baseline for general user acceptance, complex pedagogical interactions, such as those intended by the Socratic learning assistant developed in this work, cannot be adequately evaluated through isolated Q\&A pairs.
Allowing users to engage in multi-turn, goal-oriented dialogue would enable more accurate assessment of scaffolded knowledge transfer, all the while maintaining measurable experimental control and dialogic knowledge transfer. However, combining this approach with expert evaluation, which relies on multi-turn dialogue involving educational content, captures both user acceptance and educational value.

The cost analysis complements the qualitative and quantitative approaches by adding the practical relevant economic perspective to the evaluation. Recent literature highlights that system-level design choices, such as retrieval strategies or reranking, directly impact computational efficiency and latency \autocite{liBPRAG,zhaoragSurvey}. This is particularly true of learning platforms, where differences in token consumption or latency can significantly impact operating costs and the user experience. However, it should be noted that this analysis is currently indicative. A fully realistic assessment would need to account for additional factors such as varying usage patterns, amortised infrastructure costs and optimisation strategies such as prompt caching. Furthermore, it is evident that aggregated metrics inadequately capture certain indirect effects, such as savings resulting from workflows without retrieval.

In the context of educational chatbots, we demonstrated that the expert blind test was the only method capable of measuring educational quality. This method is therefore indispensable, particularly in this context. Although none of the used methods is sufficient on its own to evaluate the quality of a RAG system, combining them provides a solid foundation.

\subsection{Reflection of the Best Practice Implementation}

The results show that the architectural changes affect performance differently depending on the use case, highlighting the usual trade-off between quality gains and higher costs \autocite{liBPRAG,Neumann2024MoodleBot}. Expanding the knowledge base through improved ingestion increased contextual relevance, but in education, where content is often already well structured, more tokens do not necessarily improve answer quality and may add noise. This suggests that data structure and didactic preparation matter more than sheer volume. The routing mechanism was one of the clearest improvements. It allows simple or conversational queries to bypass retrieval, reducing cost and latency while often improving response quality by avoiding unnecessary context \autocite{liBPRAG,MultiHopRAG}. Its benefit is strongest in settings with many simple interactions.

Best-practice RAG components such as hybrid retrieval, reranking, and multi-hop retrieval improved technical retrieval quality \autocite{liBPRAG,zhaoragSurvey}, but in educational Q\&A they did not always improve perceived quality and could amount to overengineering. In contrast, the Socratic learning assistant benefited clearly from stronger grounding and richer retrieval, enabling more targeted and personalized guidance \autocite{socaraticChatbot,Shetye2024Khanmigo}. Minor UX changes, especially better citations and streaming, also improved perceived quality, consistent with prior work on usability and transparency \autocite{ZawackiRichter2019}. Overall, modern RAG practices should be applied selectively in education, balancing complexity, cost, and pedagogical value.
