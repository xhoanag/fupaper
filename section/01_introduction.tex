The ongoing digital transformation is fundamentally reshaping how knowledge is created, accessed, and communicated, opening up new opportunities for personalized and adaptive learning environments. Artificial Intelligence (AI) has assumed an increasingly central role in education, as intelligent systems are capable of supporting learners individually, tailoring learning processes, and facilitating access to complex content \autocite{ZawackiRichter2019}. In particular, conversational assistants based on Large Language Models (LLMs) have gained significant attention in both research and practice. Their ability to process natural language and generate contextually relevant responses enables a form of interactive learning support that extends beyond traditional, static educational resources \autocite{Neumann2024MoodleBot,Oates2025ChatGPTClassroom}. At the same time, the rapid adoption of LLM-based assistants highlights a critical limitation. Generative models rely solely on their training data and therefore risk producing inaccurate or unverifiable information. Such hallucinations are especially problematic in educational contexts, where the reliability, traceability, and correctness of information are essential. Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) has emerged as a key architectural approach to mitigate these issues by combining generative models with retrieval mechanisms that incorporate external, domain-specific knowledge into the response generation process \autocite{Lewis2020_RAG,Swacha2025}.

This paper is situated within the context of the KI-Campus platform, a large-scale, publicly accessible learning environment for AI with over 130,000 users as of early 2026. A RAG-based chatbot is already deployed on the platform which supports users in navigating course offerings and answering domain-specific questions. Despite its productive use, the current system exhibits notable shortcomings. These include limitations in its core architecture and the absence of a more pedagogically aligned learning assistant. The project therefore pursues two primary objectives. First, it aims to enhance the existing system by implementing best practices in modern RAG architectures and adding a learning assistant. Second, it addresses the lack of standardized evaluation methodologies for RAG-based systems \autocite{Yu2024RAGEvalSurvey,Swacha2025} by developing a multidimensional comparative evaluation framework. Building on these objectives, the paper contributes to two research directions: the systems engineering of educational chatbots and the comparative evaluation of such systems using a synthesized framework. The main contribution of this work is the proposal and application of a multidimensional comparative evaluation framework that captures both technical performance and user-centered quality dimensions. Based on this framework, the paper derives key findings and practical lessons for the design, implementation, and evaluation of RAG-based educational chatbots.
