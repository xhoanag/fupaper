\section{Introduction}
The ongoing digital transformation is fundamentally reshaping how knowledge is created, accessed, and communicated, opening up new opportunities for personalized and adaptive learning environments. Artificial Intelligence (AI) has assumed an increasingly central role in education, as intelligent systems are capable of supporting learners individually, tailoring learning processes, and facilitating access to complex content \autocite{ZawackiRichter2019,Feuerriegel2024}. In particular, conversational assistants based on Large Language Models (LLMs) have gained significant attention in both research and practice. Their ability to process natural language enables interactive learning support beyond traditional educational resources \autocite{Neumann2024MoodleBot,Oates2025ChatGPTClassroom}. At the same time, the rapid adoption of LLM-based assistants highlights a critical limitation. Generative models rely solely on their training data and therefore risk producing inaccurate or unverifiable information. Such hallucinations are especially problematic in educational contexts, where the reliability and correctness of information are essential. Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) has emerged as a key architectural approach to mitigate these issues by combining generative models with retrieval mechanisms that incorporate external, domain-specific knowledge into the response generation process \autocite{Lewis2020_RAG,Klesel2025,Swacha2025}.

This paper is situated within the context of the KI-Campus platform, a large-scale, publicly accessible learning environment for AI with over 130,000 users as of early 2026. A RAG-based chatbot is already deployed on the platform to support users in navigating course offerings and answering domain-specific questions. Despite its productive use, the current system exhibits notable shortcomings, particularly with regard to response quality and the lack of systematic evaluation procedures. While recent research highlights the growing importance of evaluation methods for RAG-based systems, standardized and application-oriented approaches remain limited, especially in educational contexts \autocite{Yu2024RAGEvalSurvey,Swacha2025}.

Against this background, this paper addresses the following research question: How can RAG-based educational chatbots be evaluated using a multidimensional comparative evaluation approach that captures both technical performance and user-centered quality dimensions? To address this question, the study conducts a multidimensional comparative evaluation of two RAG system designs deployed on the KI-Campus platform. The evaluation is based on a case-study-driven setup that combines automated metrics, expert evaluation, user blind assessment, and cost analysis using real interaction data. The goal is to derive practical guidelines for the evaluation of RAG systems and to identify key design trade-offs that arise in real-world educational applications. The main contribution of this work is therefore an application-oriented evaluation setup for RAG-based educational chatbots and the resulting insights into how different architectural choices affect technical performance, perceived quality, and user-perceived usefulness. Based on the empirical results, the paper derives lessons learned and recommendations for the evaluation and design of RAG systems in educational contexts.
