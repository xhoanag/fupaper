\section{Related Work on RAG-Systems}

\subsection{Best Practices in RAG-System Setup in Educational Settings}
Since the introduction of Retrieval-Augmented Generation \autocite{Lewis2020_RAG}, there has been a surge in publactions exploring possible design choices for RAG-systems \autocite{zhaoragSurvey}. Contemporary research no longer treats RAG as a single linear pipeline but rather as a family of architectures that differ across retrieval, augmentation und generation stages, evolving from naive to advanced and modular designs \autocite{gaoRAGParadigms}. In this context, Naive RAG refers to a simple linear workflow in which a fixed number of documents is retrieved and directly appended to the prompt, Advanced RAG introduces pre- and post-retrieval optimizations such as query reformulation, routing, and reranking to improve relevance, and Modular (or agent-based) RAG enables dynamic orchestration of multiple components, allowing the system to iteratively decide when and how to retrieve, refine, or generate based on the query and intermediate results \autocite{gaoRAGParadigms}. Across this literature, retrieval quality is consistently identified as a key performance driver, often outweighing pure model scaling \autocite{liBPRAG}. Hybrid retrieval approaches that combine dense semantic representations with lexical techniques improve performance metrices across heterogeneous corpora, while reranking models furhter refine retrieved documents and reduce irrelevant context \autocite{liBPRAG}. Additional optimization strategies such as query expansion, adaptive retrieval frequency, and chunking design significantly influence response quality and efficiency, highlighting the importance of system-level design choices beyond the underlying model \autocite{zhaoragSurvey}. Moving beyond static pipelines, recent work introduces adaptive and self-reflective (agent-like) architectures such as SELF-RAG, which dynamically determine whether retrieval is necessary and evaluate both retrieved evidence and generated outputs which improves factual consistency and citation quality \autocite{SelfRAG}. Such developments are conceptually aligned with reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF), which has been shown to improve alignment and response quality in language models by incorporating human preferences into optimization processes \autocite{RLHF}.
A central challenge in modern RAG systems is the handling of complex queries requiring multi-hop reasoning, where relevant information must be aggregated from multiple documents \autocite{MultiHopRAG}. This has led to the development of iterative retrieval strategies, query decomposition, and modular pipelines that dynamically orchestrate retrieval and reasoning steps \autocite{MultiHopRAG}. Graph-based approaches, commonly referred to as GraphRAG, represent a further evolution by structuring knowledge as graphs to enable relational reasoning and global corpus understanding \autocite{GraphRAG_Grundlage}. These systems construct entity-relation graphs and use hierarchical summarization techniques to answer queries that require a holistic understanding of large document collections \autocite{GraphRAG_Grundlage}. Comparative studies suggest that while GraphRAG can provide advantages for reasoning-intensive and multi-hop queries compared to traditional RAG, these benefits come with notable trade-offs, including increased computational overhead for graph construction and maintenance, higher storage requirements, and sensitivity to graph quality \autocite{GraphRAG_Evaluation}. Recent work further extends RAG to multimodal settings. Multimodal RAG (MRAG) integrates data such as images, audio, and video into the retrieval and generation process, enabling richer contextual grounding and improved performance in complex tasks \autocite{MMRAG}.

In the educational domain, RAG-based chatbots are increasingly explored as tools to support learning processes, particularly for question answering and knowledge access. Empirical implementations show that such systems can reliably support learners, although many interactions remain administrative rather than pedagogically deep \autocite{MARK}. Similar findings are reported for iterative RAG-based systems, where reranking and iterative retrieval improve response quality but do not fully leverage the pedagogical potential of conversational agents \autocite{ScalaBot_Soliman}. But even RAG Systems that did not use sophisticated approaches such as MoodleBot demonstrate that chatbots can support both answering and generating questions, achieving measurable user acceptance in educational settings \autocite{Neumann2024MoodleBot}. A key research direction is the integration of pedagogical interaction strategies. Socratic chatbot approaches guide learners through targeted questioning rather than direct answers, thereby fostering reflection, critical thinking, and active engagement \autocite{socaraticChatbot}. This approach is already used by major commercial systems such as Google's Gemini Tutor, DuoLingo's Max, and Khan Academy's Khanmigo. Studies Khanmigo, indicate that conversational agents can enable personalized learning experiences when designed to scaffold reasoning processes instead of simply delivering answers \autocite{Shetye2024Khanmigo}.
