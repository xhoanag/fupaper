\subsection{Best Practices in RAG-System Setup in Educational Settings}
Since the introduction of Retrieval-Augmented Generation \autocite{Lewis2020_RAG}, there has been a surge in publications exploring possible design choices for RAG-systems \autocite{zhaoragSurvey}. Contemporary research no longer treats RAG as a single linear pipeline but rather as a family of architectures that differ across retrieval, augmentation and generation stages, evolving from naive to advanced and modular designs \autocite{gaoRAGParadigms}. In this context, Naive RAG refers to a simple linear workflow in which a fixed number of documents is retrieved and directly appended to the prompt, Advanced RAG introduces pre- and post-retrieval optimizations such as contextualization of queries, routing as well as reranking to improve relevance and Modular (or agent-based) RAG enables dynamic orchestration of multiple components, allowing the system to iteratively decide when and how to retrieve, refine, or generate based on the query and intermediate results \autocite{gaoRAGParadigms}. Across this literature, retrieval quality is consistently identified as a key performance driver, often outweighing pure model scaling \autocite{liBPRAG}. Hybrid retrieval approaches that combine dense semantic representations with lexical techniques improve performance metrics across heterogeneous corpora, while reranking models further refine retrieved documents and reduce irrelevant context \autocite{liBPRAG}. Additional optimization strategies such as query expansion, adaptive retrieval frequency, and chunking design significantly influence response quality and efficiency, highlighting the importance of system-level design choices beyond the underlying model \autocite{zhaoragSurvey}. Moving beyond static pipelines, recent work introduces adaptive and self-reflective (agent-like) architectures such as SELF-RAG, which dynamically determine whether retrieval is necessary and evaluate both retrieved evidence and generated outputs which improves factual consistency and citation quality \autocite{SelfRAG}. Such developments are conceptually aligned with reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF), which has been shown to improve response quality in LLMs by incorporating human preferences \autocite{RLHF}.
A central challenge in modern RAG systems is the handling of complex queries requiring multi-hop reasoning, where relevant information must be aggregated from multiple documents \autocite{MultiHopRAG}. This has led to the development of iterative retrieval strategies, query decomposition and modular pipelines that dynamically orchestrate retrieval and reasoning steps \autocite{MultiHopRAG}. Graph-based approaches, referred to as GraphRAG, represent a further evolution by structuring knowledge as graphs to enable relational reasoning and global corpus understanding \autocite{GraphRAG_Grundlage}. These systems construct entity-relation graphs and use hierarchical summarization techniques to answer queries that require a holistic understanding of large document collections \autocite{GraphRAG_Grundlage}. Comparative studies suggest that while GraphRAG can provide advantages for reasoning-intensive and multi-hop queries compared to traditional RAG, these benefits come with notable trade-offs. These include increased computational overhead for graph construction and maintenance, higher storage requirements and sensitivity to graph quality \autocite{GraphRAG_Evaluation}. Recent work further extends RAG to multimodal settings. Multimodal RAG (MRAG) integrates data such as images, audio, and video into the retrieval and generation process which enables richer contextual grounding and improved performance in complex tasks \autocite{MMRAG}.

In the educational domain, RAG-based chatbots are increasingly explored as tools to support learning processes. Empirical implementations show that such systems can reliably support learners, although many interactions remain administrative rather than pedagogically deep \autocite{MARK}. Similar findings are reported for iterative RAG-based systems, where reranking and iterative retrieval improve response quality but do not fully leverage the pedagogical potential of conversational agents \autocite{ScalaBot_Soliman}. But even RAG Systems that did not use sophisticated approaches such as MoodleBot demonstrate that chatbots can support both answering and generating questions, achieving measurable user acceptance in educational settings \autocite{Neumann2024MoodleBot}. A key research direction is the integration of pedagogical interaction strategies. Socratic chatbot approaches guide learners through targeted questioning rather than direct answers, thereby fostering reflection, critical thinking, and active engagement \autocite{socaraticChatbot}. This approach is already used by major commercial systems such as Google's Gemini Tutor, DuoLingo's Max, and Khan Academy's Khanmigo. Studies on the Khanmigo indicate that conversational agents can enable personalized learning experiences when designed to scaffold reasoning processes instead of simply delivering answers \autocite{Shetye2024Khanmigo}. The literature thus demonstrates a progression from simple question-and-answer (Q\&A) scenarios to interactive learning support in educational settings. Such complex RAG systems are already in productive use but have not yet been extensively studied in the literature.

\subsection{RAG-System Evaluation in Educational Settings}

Evaluating Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) systems is challenging because overall performance depends on both retrieval quality and downstream generation, as well as their interaction. Early RAG work was primarily evaluated on knowledge-intensive question answering benchmarks with reference answers, enabling standardized correctness measures but only partially reflecting real-world deployments with dynamic and domain-specific knowledge bases \autocite{Lewis2020_RAG}.

Recent research therefore conceptualizes RAG evaluation as a multi-stage problem. Retrieval quality is commonly assessed using established information retrieval metrics such as Precision@k, Recall@k, and Mean Reciprocal Rank \autocite{manning2008ir, karpukhin2020dpr}, while generation quality is evaluated in terms of relevance and factual consistency with the retrieved context. In this regard, \textit{faithfulness} has emerged as a central criterion, capturing whether generated claims are supported by retrieved evidence \autocite{es2025ragas}. However, empirical studies show that hallucinations can still occur in RAG systems despite access to external knowledge, which necessitates explicit evaluation of factual consistency.

Beyond component-wise evaluation, recent work highlights that evaluating RAG systems solely on question answering tasks is insufficient.
For instance, \autocite{Deriu2021} emphasize that static, single-turn evaluations fail to capture the complexities of multi-turn interactions, arguing instead for task-oriented dialogue assessments.
Benchmarks such as RGB assess additional capabilities including robustness to noisy or misleading context, information integration across multiple documents, and the ability to abstain when evidence is insufficient \autocite{chen2023benchmarkinglargelanguagemodels}. Similarly, CRUD-RAG extends evaluation to diverse application scenarios beyond question answering and considers the impact of different RAG components across tasks \autocite{CRUDRAG}. These developments indicate that RAG evaluation must account for broader usage patterns and system-level behavior rather than isolated component performance.

To address scalability challenges, automated evaluation approaches using large language models (LLMs) as judges have gained prominence. Frameworks such as RAGAS and ARES enable (largely) reference-free or weakly supervised evaluation using metrics such as answer relevance and faithfulness, reducing the need for costly ground-truth annotations \autocite{es2025ragas, saadfalcon2024ares}. While LLM-based evaluation can correlate with human judgments in certain settings, it is sensitive to prompting and prone to systematic biases, limiting its reliability as a standalone method.

Consequently, human evaluation remains essential, particularly for domain-specific applications, where expert knowledge and qualitative judgment are required. Hybrid approaches that combine automated metrics with human or expert-based evaluation are therefore increasingly recommended to leverage the scalability of automated methods while capturing nuanced quality aspects that automated approaches may miss \autocite{bdcc9120320, 202504.0369}.

In educational contexts, evaluation extends beyond technical correctness to include pedagogical quality and user-centered outcomes. Empirical studies on LLM-based educational chatbots show that evaluation commonly includes both response accuracy and user acceptance, for example through constructs such as perceived usefulness and ease of use \autocite{Neumann2024MoodleBot}. Recent work on RAG-based educational chatbots additionally combines metrics such as faithfulness, relevance, latency, and human evaluation within specific system evaluations, highlighting the potential of multidimensional assessment in practical settings \autocite{khan2025ragbenchmark}.

Overall, the literature highlights the need for multidimensional, application-oriented evaluation approaches that integrate retrieval performance, generation quality, and human-centered assessment. However, several limitations remain. In particular, existing work relies predominantly on standardized benchmark datasets, with limited use of application-oriented datasets reflecting real user interactions. Furthermore, there is limited work that systematically combines automated evaluation metrics with human judgment and economic considerations such as cost and latency. Finally, comparative evaluations of different RAG system designs within a unified evaluation approach remain scarce. These gaps motivate the development of the multidimensional comparative evaluation approach proposed in this work.
