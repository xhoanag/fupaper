\subsection{Chatbots in Educational Settings}
Research on chatbots in educational contexts within the information systems literature shows a shift from rule-based and highly structured conversational agents toward data-driven and increasingly knowledge-integrating systems \autocite{Waldner2022, Chen2025,Klesel2025}. Earlier work has focused primarily on rule-based or template-driven so-called Pedagogical Conversational Agents (PCAs), which were mainly used for structured tasks such as knowledge quizzes, quiz functions, or administrative support \autocite{Waldner2022}. These systems offered a high degree of control and transparency but were limited in their ability to process context and provide personalized support. Accordingly, empirical studies show that while they can improve test performance, they do not necessarily promote deeper learning processes or sustained interaction \autocite{Waldner2022}. As data-driven models have become more widely available, the focus has shifted toward tutor-like systems that are more deeply integrated into the learning process. In these systems, chatbots take on roles such as tutor \autocite{KhosrawiRad2024} or coach \autocite{Chen2022} supporting learners through adaptive assistance or motivational interactions \autocite{Schlimbach2023}. Studies show that role-based designs in particular—such as tutor versus teammate—have a significant impact on motivation and learning behavior \autocite{KhosrawiRad2024}. Therefore, a more didactically oriented understanding of chatbots is taking hold, in which the focus is no longer solely on providing information but on actively supporting cognitive processes.

A key approach in this context is what is known as Socratic design, in which chatbots ask targeted questions to encourage reflection and independent thinking \autocite{socaraticChatbot}. This approach is explicitly discussed as a counter-model to purely response-based systems, as it reduces the risk of excessive reliance on AI-generated solutions and supports deeper learning processes \autocite{Ma2024}. At the same time, this approach places greater demands on the underlying system architecture, as relevant information must be retrieved in a context-sensitive manner and embedded in dialogic interactions. This approach is already used by major commercial systems such as Google's Gemini Tutor, DuoLingo's Max, and Khan Academy's Khanmigo. Studies on the Khanmigo indicate that conversational agents can enable personalized learning experiences when designed to scaffold reasoning processes instead of simply delivering answers \autocite{Shetye2024Khanmigo}. The literature thus demonstrates a progression from simple question-and-answer (Q\&A) scenarios to interactive learning support in educational settings.

These requirements are increasingly being implemented technically through Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), which has seen a surge in publications \autocite{zhaoragSurvey}. RAG combines parametric knowledge from language models with non-parametric, external knowledge, thereby enabling the integration of domain-specific content into the generation process \autocite{Lewis2020_RAG}. In an educational context, this is implemented in particular through course-specific chatbots that use learning materials as a knowledge base and thereby generate contextualized responses. Empirical studies show that such systems increase interaction between learners and learning content and contribute to improved perceived learning outcomes \autocite{Chen2025}. RAG is therefore increasingly viewed as an architecture that integrates organization-specific knowledge into generative systems, thereby supporting both knowledge management processes and application-oriented systems such as learning and support systems \autocite{Klesel2025,Chen2025}.

Recent research further conceptualizes RAG not as a single pipeline but as a design space of alternative architectures that differ across retrieval, augmentation, and generation stages \autocite{zhaoragSurvey}. A common distinction is made between naive, advanced, and modular RAG systems. Naive RAG follows a linear pipeline in which a fixed set of documents is retrieved and directly appended to the prompt. While straightforward to implement, its effectiveness is highly dependent on retrieval quality. Advanced RAG extends this paradigm through techniques such as hybrid retrieval, query rewriting, and reranking, which improve the relevance and quality of retrieved information. Empirical evidence shows that retrieval quality is a critical performance driver, often outweighing improvements achieved through model scaling alone \autocite{liBPRAG}. Furthermore, system-level design decisions—such as chunking strategies, query expansion, and adaptive retrieval—have a significant impact on overall system performance and efficiency \autocite{zhaoragSurvey}.

Moving beyond static pipelines, modular RAG architectures enable dynamic orchestration of multiple components, allowing systems to iteratively retrieve, reason, and refine outputs. This is particularly relevant for complex queries requiring multi-hop reasoning, where relevant information must be aggregated across multiple sources \autocite{MultiHopRAG}. Such approaches often rely on query decomposition and iterative retrieval strategies to support more advanced reasoning capabilities \autocite{MultiHopRAG}. A further extension is GraphRAG, which structures knowledge as graphs to capture relationships between entities and enable more holistic reasoning over large document collections \autocite{GraphRAG_Grundlage}. While this approach offers advantages for reasoning-intensive tasks, it introduces additional computational complexity and design trade-offs, particularly regarding graph construction and maintenance \autocite{GraphRAG_Grundlage}.

\subsection{RAG System Evaluation in Educational Settings}

Evaluating Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) systems is challenging because overall performance depends on both retrieval quality and downstream generation, as well as their interaction. Early RAG work was primarily evaluated on knowledge-intensive question answering benchmarks with reference answers, enabling standardized correctness measures but only partially reflecting real-world deployments with dynamic and domain-specific knowledge bases \autocite{Lewis2020_RAG}.

Recent research therefore conceptualizes RAG evaluation as a multi-stage problem. Retrieval quality is commonly assessed using established information retrieval metrics such as Precision@k, Recall@k, and Mean Reciprocal Rank \autocite{manning2008ir, karpukhin2020dpr}, while generation quality is evaluated in terms of relevance and factual consistency with the retrieved context. In this regard, \textit{faithfulness} has emerged as a central criterion, capturing whether generated claims are supported by retrieved evidence \autocite{es2025ragas}. However, empirical studies show that hallucinations can still occur in RAG systems despite access to external knowledge, which necessitates explicit evaluation of factual consistency.

Beyond component-wise evaluation, recent work highlights that evaluating RAG systems solely on question answering tasks is insufficient.
For instance, \autocite{Deriu2021} emphasize that static, single-turn evaluations fail to capture the complexities of multi-turn interactions, arguing instead for task-oriented dialogue assessments.
Benchmarks such as RGB assess additional capabilities including robustness to noisy or misleading context, information integration across multiple documents, and the ability to abstain when evidence is insufficient \autocite{chen2023benchmarkinglargelanguagemodels}. Similarly, CRUD-RAG extends evaluation to diverse application scenarios beyond question answering and considers the impact of different RAG components across tasks \autocite{CRUDRAG}. These developments indicate that RAG evaluation must account for broader usage patterns and system-level behavior rather than isolated component performance.

To address scalability challenges, automated evaluation approaches using large language models (LLMs) as judges have gained prominence. Frameworks such as RAGAS and ARES enable (largely) reference-free or weakly supervised evaluation using metrics such as answer relevance and faithfulness, reducing the need for costly ground-truth annotations \autocite{es2025ragas, saadfalcon2024ares}. While LLM-based evaluation can correlate with human judgments in certain settings, it is sensitive to prompting and prone to systematic biases, limiting its reliability as a standalone method.

Consequently, human evaluation remains essential, particularly for domain-specific applications, where expert knowledge and qualitative judgment are required. Hybrid approaches that combine automated metrics with human or expert-based evaluation are therefore increasingly recommended to leverage the scalability of automated methods while capturing nuanced quality aspects that automated approaches may miss \autocite{bdcc9120320, 202504.0369}.

In educational contexts, evaluation extends beyond technical correctness to include pedagogical quality and user-centered outcomes. Empirical studies on LLM-based educational chatbots show that evaluation commonly includes both response accuracy and user acceptance, for example through constructs such as perceived usefulness and ease of use \autocite{Neumann2024MoodleBot}. Recent work on RAG-based educational chatbots additionally combines metrics such as faithfulness, relevance, latency, and human evaluation within specific system evaluations, highlighting the potential of multidimensional assessment in practical settings \autocite{khan2025ragbenchmark}.

Overall, the literature highlights the need for multidimensional, application-oriented evaluation approaches that integrate retrieval performance, generation quality, and human-centered assessment. However, several limitations remain. In particular, existing work relies predominantly on standardized benchmark datasets, with limited use of application-oriented datasets reflecting real user interactions. Furthermore, there is limited work that systematically combines automated evaluation metrics with human judgment and economic considerations such as cost and latency. Finally, comparative evaluations of different RAG system designs within a unified evaluation approach remain scarce. These gaps motivate the development of the multidimensional comparative evaluation approach proposed in this work.
